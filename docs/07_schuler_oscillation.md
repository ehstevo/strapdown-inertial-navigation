# Schuler Oscillation and INS Error Dynamics

## Overview

A defining characteristic of Earth-referenced inertial navigation systems is the presence of **Schuler oscillation**, a natural error mode arising from Earth curvature and local-level navigation geometry.

This experiment investigates how sensor biases and initialization errors propagate through a stationary strapdown inertial navigation system. Although the aircraft remains motionless on the ground, small errors in the navigation state and inertial measurements cause the estimated trajectory to drift.

The resulting motion reveals how position, velocity, and attitude errors interact through the mechanization equations and demonstrates the classical Schuler response.

---

# Objectives

This experiment aims to:

- Investigate the effects of accelerometer and gyroscope biases.
- Investigate the effects of initialization errors.
- Observe Schuler oscillation in a practical INS simulation.
- Develop intuition for how attitude errors create position errors.
- Understand the role of Earth curvature in INS error dynamics.

---

# Background

## Stationary Aircraft Scenario

A stationary aircraft is placed at:

| Parameter | Value |
|------------|--------|
| Latitude | 39.535° |
| Longitude | -76.348° |
| Height Above Ellipsoid | 212 m |
| Duration | 6 hours |
| Sample Period | 1 s |

The aircraft remains motionless for the entire simulation.

A perfect inertial navigation system would estimate:

- Constant position
- Zero velocity
- Constant attitude

Any observed motion is therefore caused entirely by sensor biases or navigation-state errors.

---

## What is Schuler Oscillation?

The classical Schuler period is

$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{R_E}{g}}$

which evaluates to approximately:

**84.4 minutes**

for the Earth.

### Physical Interpretation

Schuler oscillation arises because the navigation frame is constrained to remain tangent to the Earth's surface.

Position errors create velocity errors.

Velocity errors alter the transport rate.

Transport-rate errors alter attitude.

Attitude errors alter the projection of gravity.

The resulting feedback loop creates an oscillatory response rather than simple linear drift.

---

# Simulation Setup

The forward mechanization includes:

- Earth rotation
- Transport rate
- Earth curvature
- Somigliana gravity
- DCM attitude propagation
- Modified Gram-Schmidt orthonormalization
- Ideal altitude aiding

Altitude aiding prevents vertical divergence and allows the experiment to focus on horizontal navigation errors.

# Sensor Bias Cases

## Case 1: Accelerometer X-Axis Bias

![Accelerometer X Bias](../results/figures/schuler_oscillation_demo/accel_x_bias_xy.pdf)

### Observation

The trajectory exhibits horizontal drift with a superimposed oscillatory response.

### Interpretation

A constant accelerometer bias produces a false acceleration:

```text
Accelerometer Bias
        ↓
Velocity Error
        ↓
Position Error
        ↓
Schuler Oscillation
```

Because the navigation solution is Earth-referenced, the resulting motion excites the Schuler mode.

---

## Case 2: Accelerometer Y-Axis Bias

![Accelerometer Y Bias](../results/figures/schuler_oscillation_demo/accel_y_bias_xy.pdf)

### Observation

The trajectory exhibits behavior similar to Case 1 but along the orthogonal horizontal axis.

### Interpretation

The mechanism is identical to Case 1. The induced velocity error excites the Earth-referenced feedback dynamics and produces Schuler oscillation.

---

## Case 3: Gyroscope X-Axis Bias

![Gyroscope X Bias](../results/figures/schuler_oscillation_demo/gyro_x_bias_xy.pdf)

### Observation

The trajectory exhibits a ripple superimposed on an overall growth trend.

### Why?

The gyroscope bias produces a continuously growing roll error.

The resulting attitude error causes gravity to be projected incorrectly into the horizontal plane:

$a_h \approx g\theta$

The ripple corresponds to Schuler oscillation.

The overall growth occurs because the gyro bias continuously injects additional attitude error into the system.

---

## Case 4: Gyroscope Y-Axis Bias

![Gyroscope Y Bias](../results/figures/schuler_oscillation_demo/gyro_y_bias_xy.pdf)

### Observation

A similar ripple-and-growth pattern is observed.

### Interpretation

The pitch error generated by the gyro bias continually misprojects gravity and drives horizontal acceleration errors.

---

## Case 5: Gyroscope Z-Axis Bias

![Gyroscope Z Bias](../results/figures/schuler_oscillation_demo/gyro_z_bias_xy.pdf)

### Observation

The resulting position error is significantly smaller than the roll and pitch cases.

### Interpretation

Yaw errors do not directly tilt gravity.

The position drift therefore occurs through weaker Earth-rotation and transport-rate coupling mechanisms.

# Initial Condition Cases

## Case 6: Initial Latitude Error

![Initial Latitude Error](../results/figures/schuler_oscillation_demo/initial_latitude_error_xy.pdf)

### Observation

The trajectory exhibits oscillatory motion with a slowly varying drift.

### Why?

Latitude affects:

- Gravity magnitude
- Earth rotation projection
- Curvature radii
- Local navigation geometry

The mechanization therefore begins with slightly incorrect Earth-model terms, exciting Schuler dynamics.

---

## Case 7: Initial Longitude Error

![Initial Longitude Error](../results/figures/schuler_oscillation_demo/initial_longitude_error_xy.pdf)

### Observation

Little or no observable change occurs.

### Why?

Longitude does not significantly affect:

- Gravity magnitude
- Earth rotation magnitude
- Curvature radii

The mechanization therefore remains nearly unchanged.

This explains why the trajectory appears almost stationary.

---

## Case 8: Initial North Velocity Error

![Initial North Velocity Error](../results/figures/schuler_oscillation_demo/initial_vn_error_xy.pdf)

### Observation

A clear oscillatory trajectory is observed.

### Why?

The false velocity excites transport-rate errors which feed directly into the Schuler feedback loop.

---

## Case 9: Initial East Velocity Error

![Initial East Velocity Error](../results/figures/schuler_oscillation_demo/initial_ve_error_xy.pdf)

### Observation

Behavior similar to Case 8 is observed.

### Interpretation

The initial velocity error directly excites the Earth-referenced oscillatory dynamics.

---

## Case 10: Initial Roll Error

![Initial Roll Error](../results/figures/schuler_oscillation_demo/initial_roll_error_xy.pdf)

### Observation

One of the largest position errors in the experiment is observed.

### Why?

The roll error immediately tilts gravity.

The INS therefore interprets part of gravity as horizontal acceleration.

Even a small roll error produces a persistent false acceleration that drives large navigation errors.

---

## Case 11: Initial Pitch Error

![Initial Pitch Error](../results/figures/schuler_oscillation_demo/initial_pitch_error_xy.pdf)

### Observation

Behavior similar to the roll-error case is observed.

### Interpretation

Pitch errors also directly misproject gravity into the horizontal plane.

These cases demonstrate that inertial navigation accuracy is fundamentally tied to attitude accuracy.

---

## Case 12: Initial Yaw Error

![Initial Yaw Error](../results/figures/schuler_oscillation_demo/initial_yaw_error_xy.pdf)

### Observation

The trajectory exhibits weak oscillatory behavior with much smaller growth than the roll and pitch cases.

### Why?

Yaw errors do not directly tilt gravity.

Position errors arise only through weaker coupling with Earth rotation and transport-rate dynamics.

# Error Magnitude Comparison

![Case Summary](../results/figures/schuler_oscillation_demo/schuler_case_summary.pdf)

### Major Trends

Several clear trends emerge:

- Gyroscope biases are generally more damaging than accelerometer biases and attitude errors.
- Roll and pitch errors produce the largest navigation errors after gyroscope biases.
- Latitude errors excite Schuler dynamics while longitude errors do not.
- Yaw errors are comparatively benign.
- Gravity misprojection is the dominant mechanism driving horizontal position drift.

# Additional Insights

## Why Do Gyroscope Errors Affect Position?

Gyroscopes do not directly measure position.

However, attitude errors alter the transformation between body-frame accelerations and navigation-frame accelerations:

```text
Gyro Error
      ↓
Attitude Error
      ↓
Acceleration Error
      ↓
Velocity Error
      ↓
Position Error
```

This coupling explains why small gyro errors can eventually produce large position drift.

---

## Why Does Earth Curvature Matter?

If Earth curvature and transport-rate terms are removed:

- Schuler oscillation disappears.
- Position errors tend to grow monotonically.

Earth curvature therefore provides the restoring mechanism responsible for the classical 84-minute oscillation.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Schuler oscillation is a fundamental error mode of Earth-referenced inertial navigation.
- The Schuler period is approximately 84.4 minutes.
- Accelerometer biases directly create velocity and position drift.
- Gyroscope biases create attitude errors that misproject gravity.
- Roll and pitch errors are generally much more damaging than yaw errors.
- Latitude errors alter Earth-model terms and excite Schuler dynamics.
- Longitude errors have little effect on local-level navigation.
- Earth curvature provides the restoring mechanism that converts drift into oscillation.
- Accurate attitude estimation is critical for long-term navigation accuracy.

This experiment provides intuition for one of the most important concepts in inertial navigation and illustrates the deep connection between attitude estimation, Earth modeling, and long-term INS performance.
