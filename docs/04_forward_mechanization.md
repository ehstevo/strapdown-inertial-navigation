# Forward Mechanization: Reconstructing Trajectory from IMU Measurements

## Overview

This experiment validates the strapdown inertial navigation mechanization equations by reconstructing an aircraft trajectory using only:

- initial position,
- initial attitude,
- initial velocity,
- accelerometer measurements,
- and gyroscope measurements.

The IMU measurements used in this experiment were generated by the inverse mechanization process described in the previous section.

The objective is to demonstrate that the forward navigation equations can accurately recover the original trajectory when provided with ideal sensor measurements and a correct initial state.

This experiment completes the full inertial navigation simulation loop:

```text
Truth Trajectory
       ↓
Inverse Mechanization
       ↓
Synthetic IMU Measurements
       ↓
Forward Mechanization
       ↓
Reconstructed Trajectory
       ↓
Comparison with Truth
```

---

# Background

## What is Forward Mechanization?

Forward mechanization is the core propagation process used by a strapdown inertial navigation system.

At each time step, the navigation solution is updated using:

- accelerometer measurements,
- gyroscope measurements,
- Earth rotation,
- gravity,
- and Earth curvature.

The mechanization continuously estimates:

- position,
- velocity,
- and attitude.

Unlike inverse mechanization, which generates sensor measurements from a known trajectory, forward mechanization attempts to reconstruct the trajectory using only the IMU measurements.

---

# Experiment Setup

## Initial Conditions

The aircraft begins at:

- Latitude: 39.535°
- Longitude: -76.348°
- Height Above Ellipsoid: 212 m

The simulation duration is:

- 6 minutes
- sampling period of 0.1 seconds

The initial velocity and attitude are assumed to be known exactly.

In addition, ideal altitude aiding is provided throughout the
simulation, representing an idealized barometric altitude measurement.

---

## Input Data

The IMU data used in this experiment is generated by the inverse mechanization example.

Each sample contains:

```math
[f_{b,bi,x}, f_{b,bi,y}, f_{b,bi,z},
\omega_{b,bi,x}, \omega_{b,bi,y}, \omega_{b,bi,z},
v_{ne,x}, v_{ne,y}, v_{ne,z}]
```

where:

- $f_{b,bi}$ are body-frame specific force measurements,
- $\omega_{b,bi}$ are body-frame angular-rate measurements,
- $v_{ne}$ is the navigation-frame velocity truth.

---

# Altitude Aiding

This experiment includes ideal altitude aiding during forward
mechanization.

The full truth trajectory is passed into the mechanization algorithm,
but only the height-above-ellipsoid component is used during propagation.

Conceptually, this serves as an idealized barometric altimeter
measurement.

The motivation for this simplification is that vertical-channel inertial
navigation errors often grow significantly faster than horizontal
position errors. In many practical navigation systems, external altitude
measurements are available from:

- barometric altimeters,
- radar altimeters,
- terrain databases,
- or other aiding sources.

Providing altitude aiding allows this experiment to focus on validating
the core mechanization equations without the vertical channel dominating
the error behavior.

Importantly, the mechanization does not use truth latitude, longitude,
attitude, or velocity information during propagation. Only the altitude
measurement is externally supplied.

# Forward Mechanization Algorithm

At each time step, the mechanization performs four major updates.

---

## 1. Earth Rotation

The Earth rotation rate is computed in the navigation frame:

$\omega_{n,ei}$

This term accounts for the Earth's rotation beneath the vehicle.

Although small, Earth rotation accumulates over time and must be included for accurate inertial navigation.

---

## 2. Navigation Frame Transport Rate

As the vehicle moves across the Earth's curved surface, the local navigation frame rotates.

The navigation transport rate is:

$\omega_{n,ne}$

This quantity depends on:

- latitude,
- altitude,
- and vehicle velocity.

The transport rate becomes increasingly important for long-duration navigation.

---

## 3. Velocity Propagation

The navigation velocity is updated using:

```math
\dot{\mathbf{v}}_{ne}
=
C_{nb}\mathbf{f}_{b,bi}
-
(2\omega_{n,ei}+\omega_{n,ne})
\times
\mathbf{v}_{ne}
+
\gamma_{nl}
```

where:

- $C_{nb}$ rotates body-frame measurements into the navigation frame,
- the cross-product term contains Coriolis and transport-rate effects,
- and $\gamma_{nl}\$ is local gravity.

This equation forms the core navigation dynamics.

---

## 4. Position Propagation

The geodetic position is updated using the navigation velocity and local Earth radii of curvature.

Because latitude and longitude are angular quantities, Earth curvature must be considered when converting between angular and linear motion.

---

## 5. Attitude Propagation

The body attitude is updated using gyroscope measurements.

The body rotation relative to the navigation frame is:

```math
\omega_{b,bn}
=
\omega_{b,bi}
-
C_{nb}^T
(
\omega_{n,ei}
+
\omega_{n,ne}
)
```

The resulting incremental rotation is applied using Rodrigues' rotation formula.

The direction cosine matrix (DCM) is then orthonormalized using modified Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization to prevent numerical drift.

# Results and Discussion

## 1. Reconstructed Geodetic Trajectory

The first figure shows the reconstructed aircraft trajectory obtained solely from IMU propagation.

![Reconstructed Trajectory](../results/figures/forward_mechanization_demo/reconstructed_geodetic_trajectory.pdf)

### Observation

The reconstructed trajectory closely follows the original figure-eight path.

This demonstrates that the mechanization equations correctly recover the aircraft motion when provided with ideal sensor measurements.

Any visible discrepancy is due to numerical integration and finite-difference approximations.

---

## 2. Geodetic Position Errors

The following figure compares the reconstructed and truth geodetic trajectories.

![Geodetic Errors](../results/figures/forward_mechanization_demo/geodetic_error.pdf)

### Observation

Latitude, longitude, and altitude errors remain very small throughout the simulation.

This result validates:

- Earth curvature modeling,
- velocity propagation,
- and position integration.

The small error growth is expected because the mechanization uses discrete-time integration.

---

## 3. Attitude Errors

The next figure compares the reconstructed and truth roll, pitch, and yaw angles.

![Attitude Errors](../results/figures/forward_mechanization_demo/attitude_error.pdf)

### Observation

The attitude solution remains extremely close to the truth trajectory.

This indicates that:

- Earth-rate compensation,
- transport-rate compensation,
- Rodrigues attitude updates,
- and DCM orthonormalization

are all functioning correctly.

Because ideal gyroscope measurements are used, attitude errors remain minimal.

---

## 4. Velocity Errors

The final figure compares the reconstructed and truth navigation-frame velocities.

![Velocity Errors](../results/figures/forward_mechanization_demo/velocity_error.pdf)

### Observation

Velocity errors remain small over the entire simulation.

This validates the implementation of:

- Coriolis corrections,
- transport-rate corrections,
- gravity compensation,
- and navigation-frame acceleration propagation.

The velocity solution serves as the bridge between attitude and position propagation, making it one of the most important components of the mechanization.

# Validation of Inverse Mechanization

One of the primary goals of this experiment is to validate the inverse mechanization implementation.

The workflow is:

```text
Truth Trajectory
      ↓
Inverse Mechanization
      ↓
Ideal IMU Signals
      ↓
Forward Mechanization
      ↓
Recovered Trajectory
```

If both algorithms are implemented correctly, the recovered trajectory should closely match the original truth trajectory.

The small residual errors observed in this experiment provide confidence that:

- inverse mechanization is generating physically consistent IMU data,
- and forward mechanization is correctly implementing the navigation equations.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Forward mechanization reconstructs position, velocity, and attitude from IMU measurements.
- Earth rotation and transport-rate corrections are essential components of accurate inertial navigation.
- Accelerometer measurements must be transformed into the navigation frame and corrected for gravity and Coriolis effects.
- Gyroscope measurements must be corrected for both Earth rotation and navigation-frame rotation.
- Direction cosine matrices require periodic orthonormalization to prevent numerical drift.
- The reconstructed trajectory closely matches the truth trajectory, validating both the inverse and forward mechanization implementations under ideal altitude aiding.

This experiment forms the foundation for the remaining studies involving:

- sensor noise,
- INS error propagation,
- Schuler oscillation,
- static alignment,
- and GPS/INS integration.
